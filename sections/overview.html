Overview

Understanding how a sense of self emerges has been a central question in cognitive science for decades. Through a combination of controlled behavioral experiments and computational theories, researchers have investigated how humans identify themselves, discover their own agency, distinguish self-generated actions from external events, and construct internal representations of their bodies. Inspired by these foundations, we introduce two complementary paradigms for evaluating self-centric intelligence in embodied MLLMs: Experiment Translation, which transfers classical cognitive science experiments into embodied AI environments, and Theory Translation, which operationalizes influential cognitive theories as evaluation frameworks. Together, these benchmarks assess multiple dimensions of self-centric intelligence, ranging from primitive sensorimotor agency and body-schema formation to explicit self-recognition and self-referential reasoning.

Cognitive Science Background Evaluation Framework
Mirror Self-Recognition Test (Gallup, 1970; Rochat, 2003)
A classic paradigm in comparative psychology and developmental science. In the mark test, a visible mark is secretly placed on an individual's body; successful recognition is demonstrated when the individual uses a mirror to inspect or touch the mark on itself rather than on the reflected image.
MirrorBench
We extend the classical Mirror Self-Recognition paradigm to embodied physical simulation, enabling the evaluation of self-centric intelligence in MLLMs. The benchmark features a four-level protocol spanning mirror perception, mirror reasoning, mirror discovery, and explicit self-recognition.
Contingency Detection Theory (Watson, 1972; Gergely & Watson, 1999)
Early agency emerges when infants discover consistent action-effect contingencies between their own movements and environmental changes.
SAM – Gridworld-2D
Measures primitive agency discovery by requiring agents to identify which entity is under their control through sensorimotor interaction.
Comparator Model Theory (Wolpert et al., 1995; Frith et al., 2000)
Agency arises from comparing predicted and observed consequences of actions, enabling causal attribution in complex environments.
SAM – RTS Arena
Tests self-identification under multi-agent interactions, adversarial dynamics, and causal ambiguity.
Body Schema Theory (Held & Hein, 1958; Gallagher, 2006)
The brain maintains an internal representation of the body and its movement capabilities to support embodied interaction.
SAM – Phys-3D
Evaluates body-schema formation by requiring agents to infer hidden action semantics and control a body under changing viewpoints.
